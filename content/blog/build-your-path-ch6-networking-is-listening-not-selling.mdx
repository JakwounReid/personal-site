Most people hate networking.

They imagine awkward conversations, forced self-promotion, and trying to convince strangers they're worth an opportunity. For non-traditional candidates, networking can feel even worse — like trying to justify your existence in rooms you don't belong in.

That version of networking deserves its reputation. But real networking — the kind that actually works — looks very different.

## Why Traditional Networking Advice Fails

Most advice frames networking as pitching yourself, collecting contacts, asking for favors, or "selling your personal brand."

That approach creates pressure and anxiety because it puts the focus on you proving something. In reality, effective networking shifts the focus outward. It's not about being impressive. It's about being present, curious, and useful.

## How My Opportunities Actually Came Together

Some of the most meaningful connections in my journey didn't come from cold outreach or perfectly crafted pitches. They came from:

- Listening closely to people share their stories
- Finding genuine points of connection
- Following up without expectations
- Showing consistency over time

During programs like i.c.stars, we regularly heard from people already working in the industry. Instead of worrying about what I should say, I focused on understanding how they got where they were, what challenges they faced, and what they valued in teammates.

Because of that, conversations felt natural — and relationships formed without force.

## Core Lesson #6: Relationships Form When People Feel Seen

People remember how you made them feel more than what you said.

When someone feels heard, they trust you faster, they're more open to helping, and they see you as a peer, not a transaction.

Listening is a skill — and it's one many technical environments undervalue but desperately need.

## How to Network Without Feeling Fake

**Replace pitching with curiosity.** Instead of preparing a pitch, prepare questions. Good questions: "How did you get into this role?" / "What skills mattered most early on?" / "What do you wish you had known starting out?" People enjoy talking about their journeys — especially when it feels genuine.

**Look for shared context.** Connection doesn't require similarity in titles. It can come from similar career pivots, shared industries, overlapping life experiences, or mutual interests. These small overlaps make conversations human instead of hierarchical.

**Follow up with intent.** A simple follow-up matters more than a flashy message: *"I appreciated hearing about your path and how you navigated early challenges. It gave me a lot to think about."* No ask. No pressure. Just acknowledgment.

## Networking as a Long Game

Most opportunities don't come from asking directly. They come later — when someone remembers you, when a role opens up, when a referral is needed. Your name comes up naturally.

This only happens when relationships aren't rushed.

## Common Networking Myths

- *"I need to be confident and polished."* Authenticity beats polish.
- *"I have nothing to offer."* Listening, follow-through, and reliability are valuable.
- *"I should only network when I need something."* That's when it feels transactional.

## Your Listening Network — Two Week Challenge

Over the next two weeks:
1. Identify 3 people whose paths interest you
2. Reach out with curiosity, not requests
3. Focus on understanding, not impressing

Track what you learn — not what you gain.

When networking becomes listening, conversations feel lighter, anxiety decreases, and opportunities feel organic. You stop chasing doors — and start noticing when they open.
